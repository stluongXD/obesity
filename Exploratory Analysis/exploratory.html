Distribution of Variables

Obesity and Poverty Rates

The boxplots above display the distribution of the obesity rates and poverty rates across all fifty states. It is clear that the distribution of obesity rates is higher than that of poverty rates. The average obesity rate across all states is higher than that of poverty rates. The mean obesity rate is 28.2% which is 14.3% higher than the poverty mean of 13.9%. In addition, the median obesity rate of 28.4% is 14.6% higher than the poverty rate median of 28.4%. This shows that, on average, a higher percent of people across the United States are obese then are in poverty. Both rates have an inner quartile range of under 5, meaning that 50% of the rates for each states are less than a 5% difference of each other. The inner quartile range for obesity rates is 4.99 and the inner quartile range for poverty rates is 4.64. The distribution of both rates can also be analized in terms of standard deviation. The standard deviation for obesity rate is 3.43 and the standard deviation for poverty rate is 2.99.

Analysis of Distribution of Average Income

In analyzing the chart, it can be shown that the average income increases each year. From 2014 to 2015, the mean average income increased around 1,069 dollars. From 2015 to 2016, the mean average income increased 929 dollars. Finally, from 2016 to 2017, the mean average income increased 1,133 dollars. The minimum and maximum average income values also increase across years. There is a relationship then, between year and income, in which average income increases each year. Only one outlier during 2014 is visible that is almost equivalent to the maximum value of 2015.

Relationships Between Variables

Effects Of Wealth and Obesity


Unsuprisingly, we see a correlation between income and obesity. In general as the average income increases, we see the obesity rate decreases as well. This isnt a surprise due to the issue that less wealthy people are more likely to live in food deserts and have less access to purchasing healthier foods. Another issue that can influence this correlation is that junk food are typically cheaper than healthier foods. We see this with fast food restaurants and how they are able to offer very cheap meals as compared to a standard restaurant.


We continue to see the trend with wealth and obesity rates as well when we compare poverty rates and obestiy rates. In this graph, we see that as poverty rates rises, so does the obesity rate. This falls in line with our conclusions with average income because income is the determining factor if someone lives in poverty or not.


Finally, comparing a states GDP to its obesity rate, we see that there isnt a strong correlation between the two. This is highly surprising because the previous two charts display a fairly strong correlation between wealth and obesity while as we see in this chart, a states GDP typically doesnt affect the obesity rates of its state. Although, we can see that the wealthier states have a relatively low obesity rate, the poorer states have a large variation of obesity rates. This draws into question if there are other factors, such as region, that can cause such a large disparity among the poor states.

Another interesting trend we see in all three charts is that the years, which are color coded on the graphs are distrubuted quite evenly. We do not see clusters of obesity and income rates for certain years which suggests that there is not a signicant change in income or obesity levels as time goes on. However, our data is limited from 2014-2017 so further research will be needed to better determine how obesity levels change with time.

Obesity Rates Across Regions


Based on this bar chart, we can see that average obesity rates across different regions from year 2014 to 2017 is at around 30%. The southeast Region has the highest average obesity rate whereas the Rocky Mountain Region has the lowest average obesity rate.

Since the higher obesity rate regions and the lower obesity rate regions are scattered, we can conclude that there is no strong correlation between region and obesity rates. One possible explaination for why southeast region has the highest obesity rate could be because that there is more urban area in the southeast region.
